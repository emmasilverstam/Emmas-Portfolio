A pillow for endometriosis

Type: Individual work

When: 2023

Duration: Four weeks, half time

Project scope: School project in Concept-Driven Interaction Design

This project used a soma design process to explore how design can promote body awareness and self-compassion during endometriosis-related pain. Through first-person exploration and somatic prototyping, it examined how warmth, weight and breathing rhythms could help reconnect with the body. The resulting design, EndoBreathe, is a soft heating pillow that synchronises light and warmth with the user’s breath. the goal with this was to create moments of calm and to be in the present.

The process

Concept portrait

To explore the lived experience of endometriosis, I created a concept portrait using symbolic imagery. Each element including a constricting garment, a marathon, a jellyfish, a hospital corridor, and a resilient fictional character, represented different emotional and physical layers of the condition. The portrait helped me communicate the complexity of chronic pain and grounded my design direction in embodied and real-life experiences.

First-person perspective

Since I live with endometriosis, I used first-person reflections from my own pain diary to understand how pain shapes presence, breath and emotional wellbeing. Writing from within the experience helped me identify recurring patterns such as disconnection, tension, shallow breathing and the need for comfort. These insights became the foundation for how the design should feel on the body.

Body mapping

I worked with body mapping in three stages, each with a different purpose. The first map was created during an actual pain episode to capture where the pain appeared, how it moved and how it affected my body. The second map was made after a somatic meditation and focused on how conscious breathing softened tension and changed my sensations during pain. The final map was created after using the prototype, allowing me to evaluate how the warmth, weight and light influenced my body and helped me feel more grounded and connected.

Sensory Prototyping

To explore which sensations supported presence and calm, I tested different materials, weights, degrees of heat and light qualities directly on my body. These early experiments revealed the importance of warmth, gentle pressure and soft textures. Sensory prototyping also guided the decision to place weight on the stomach and chest.

Love Letter

I wrote a love letter to the emerging concept to articulate the emotional role the product could play. Writing to the design as if it were a companion helped me clarify its purpose: a calm, supportive presence that stays with the user through discomfort, mirrors their state with kindness and gently guides them back into their body.

Moodboard

I created a moodboard to captures the atmosphere I wanted the design to carry: warmth, softness, closeness and a sense of being cared for. I chose images that express comfort through touch, gentle materials and calm moments. Together, they helped me shape a feeling of safety and support, the kind of presence I wish I had during pain.

Digital Sketching

I used digital sketches to quickly explore shapes, placements and interactions. These sketches helped translate insights from the somaesthetic explorations into visual forms and allowed me to iterate on different silhouettes before making the physical prototype.

Test and Mid-fidelity prototyping

In the mid-fidelity stage, I sewed an early version of the pillow, filled it with varying amounts of wheat to test weight distribution and added an RGB LED strip to explore the role of light. Sewing allowed me to iterate on thickness, softness and body fit, while the light tests helped me understand how colour and glow could support calm breathing and reflect pain levels.

The Result

The final concept, EndoBreathe, became a soft, body-shaped pillow that gives warmth, gentle weight and a calming light that follows the rhythm of the breath. It’s designed to rest over the stomach and chest, the places where pain and tension often gather and to offer a sense of grounding when everything feels overwhelming. The combination of heat, weight, and pulsing light creates a moment of quiet which helps the body feel safer and makes it easier to stay present during pain.

The Prototype

The final prototype shows how the pillow looks and feels on the body, with its soft shape, weight and light all working together. In the video, the light follows the breath while the pillow rests naturally across the stomach and chest. I tested the interaction using a Wizard-of-Oz setup to simulate the breathing-synced light, which helped me understand how warmth, weight and glow support the body during pain.
